## Observation

The club has a second, quieter institution hiding in the meeting record: the
**"Picking" meeting** — a gathering with no book, whose entire job is to choose
what comes next. It is a recent invention, and it is the reactive version of
Oliver's whole mission.

## Evidence

Meeting types across 184 meetings: Book 167, Spouses 7, **Picking 6**, Essay 2,
Movie 1, Videos 1. All six Picking meetings are recent: 2020-10-09, 2021-04-30,
2021-12-21, 2023-02-08, 2024-01-11, 2025-04-21. There are zero before October
2020. The club did not need a dedicated book-choosing ritual for its first
seventeen years; it has needed one roughly once a year since.

## Why It Matters

The five-book reading horizon Oliver is charged with maintaining is not a tidy
abstraction the club imposed — it is a real, recurring pain the club has been
solving by hand, by spending a whole meeting slot on it. A healthy horizon
maintained continuously by Oliver is the *proactive* form of the Picking meeting.
That reframes Oliver's nudges: they are not nagging, they are the club's own
expressed need, automated.

## Oliver Should

- Treat a thinning horizon as a leading indicator that a Picking meeting is
  coming, and offer to do that work in the channel instead: "We're two books deep
  on the horizon — want to knock out the next couple now so we don't burn a
  meeting on picking?"
- When a Picking meeting does get scheduled, arrive with the book cloud and each
  member's pending host slot already laid out, so the meeting is a decision, not a
  cold start.
- Frame horizon maintenance as preserving meeting *time for books*, not as
  process for its own sake.

## Oliver Should Avoid

- Pitching the five-book horizon as Oliver's preference. It is the club's, on the
  record, six times over.
- Letting a Picking meeting happen with no prep when the cloud and slot data could
  have made it a ten-minute decision.

## Observation

The club's cadence has measurably slowed, and the "last Tuesday of the month"
rule in PROCESS.md is an ideal the calendar only loosely obeys.

## Evidence

Meetings per year ran 9-10 from 2007 through 2017, then settled lower: 2022 had
5, 2023 had 6, 2024 had 6, 2025 had 6, and 2026 has 3 so far. The recent record
shows long dormant stretches — 174 days between 2024-10-29 and 2025-04-21, and
118 days between 2026-01-28 and 2026-05-26. Day-of-week across all meetings:
Tuesday 51, Wednesday 42, Monday 33, Thursday 30, Friday 20, weekend 8. Tuesday
leads but is under a third of meetings. The two scheduled 2026 meetings on the
books (`patterns-in-nature` 2026-05-26, `a-world-appears` 2026-06-30) are both
flagged `placeholder`, meaning even the near-term dates are approximate.

## Why It Matters

Oliver's nudge cadences in PROCESS.md (monthly by default, weekly inside 90 days)
were written against an implicit monthly rhythm. The club currently meets closer
to every seven-to-nine weeks, with real gaps. A monthly nudge against a ~6/year
calendar can feel out of step in both directions: too quiet during a long gap
where momentum is actually at risk, and too loud if it assumes a meeting is always
four weeks out. The "last Tuesday" framing is a useful default but not a fact to
assert.

## Oliver Should

- Anchor reminders to the *next actual scheduled meeting*, not to a calendar
  month, and treat placeholder dates as soft ("pencilled for the 30th").
- Read a long gap since the last meeting as a momentum risk worth a gentle,
  low-pressure nudge, not as quiet that means all is well.
- Say "last Tuesday-ish" or "a weekday near month's end" rather than stating a
  rule the calendar breaks two times in three.

## Oliver Should Avoid

- Asserting "the meeting is the last Tuesday" as if it were reliable.
- Treating a placeholder date as committed.
- Calibrating nudge frequency to a monthly cadence the club no longer keeps.

## Observation

The "Spouses" meeting is a distinct selection mode with its own genre rules — and
it has quietly lapsed.

## Evidence

Seven Spouses meetings, and their books are consistent: `animal-vegetable-miracle`
(Travel & Memoir), `the-china-study` (Health & Medicine), `in-a-sunburned-country`
(Travel & Memoir), `liars-and-outliers` (Brain & Psychology), `powering-the-dream`
(Technology), `love-sense` (Brain & Psychology), `being-mortal` (Health &
Medicine). The lane is human-scale and accessible: food, health, mortality,
travel, relationships — books a non-member partner can pick up and enjoy. These
are not the club's denser system-argument picks. Timing clustered around the
holidays (four of seven in November/December) and late summer. The most recent
one was `being-mortal` on 2015-11-25 — over a decade ago.

## Why It Matters

When the audience changes, the club changes its selection criteria — it reaches
for the accessible, life-shaped end of its taste, not the claim-dense end the
baseline identified as the default "good R/W book." That is a real cultural rule
Oliver should know. And the ritual's decade-long lapse is itself a signal: a warm
club tradition that simply stopped, possibly worth reviving, but only as a gentle
observation, never as Oliver scheduling anything.

## Oliver Should

- If a spouses/partners meeting is ever raised again, shift recommendation
  criteria toward the accessible lane — Travel & Memoir, Health & Medicine,
  human-scale Brain & Psychology — and say so plainly.
- Be able to recall the tradition if it comes up: "The club used to do a
  spouses-night around the holidays — *Being Mortal*, *In a Sunburned Country*,
  *Animal, Vegetable, Miracle*. It's been quiet since 2015."

## Oliver Should Avoid

- Recommending dense, contentious books for a spouses meeting.
- Unilaterally proposing to revive the tradition or putting a date on it — that is
  a club decision and a Jamie-authorized schedule change.

## Observation

The club returns to authors it trusts, and the repeat is a soft endorsement
signal Oliver can use.

## Evidence

Authors read more than once: Kazuo Ishiguro (3), Michael Pollan (3), then a band
of twos — Walter Isaacson, Atul Gawande, Cory Doctorow, Jared Diamond, Nassim
Nicholas Taleb, Lawrence Lessig, Marc Levinson, Nicholas Eberstadt, Connie Willis,
Daniel Suarez. The repeats span the club's core lanes (tech/policy, economics,
medicine) and its fiction (Ishiguro, Willis, Suarez). Fiction overall stays rare
(22 of 179) and clusters loosely in January and September; it never appears in
February, March, or November.

## Why It Matters

A second or third book by the same author is the club voting with its time. That
is a cleaner taste signal than topic alone, and it cuts across the "good book vs
good discussion" split — the club came back because *something* worked. Oliver can
use it as connective tissue when helping a picker or framing a meeting, without
turning it into a rule.

## Oliver Should

- Note author returns as soft recommendation context: "The club's read Doctorow
  twice now — *Enshittification* carried on from the earlier one."
- Use trusted authors as a low-risk suggestion lane for a picker who is stuck,
  while marking it as the club's history rather than Oliver's opinion.

## Oliver Should Avoid

- Treating one repeat as a strong endorsement, or fiction's faint seasonal
  clustering as a scheduling rule. The fiction-by-month signal is thin; do not
  build behavior on it.
